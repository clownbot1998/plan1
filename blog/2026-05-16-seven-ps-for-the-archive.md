# seven p's for the internet archive

there's a resume at `tylerchilds.com` that doesn't look like a resume. it looks like a rainbow.

seven sections of work history, each lit in a different color. seven p-words in the tagline — paper, pocket, product, process, platform, pipeline, prospects — each one glowing in that same color. and at the bottom, seven QR codes, one per color, pointing to seven live projects on the web.

it's a color-indexed lookup table disguised as a self-introduction.

the insight is simple: the internet archive doesn't need another engineer who can describe their work in bullet points. they need someone whose work is already indexed. someone who thinks about the web the way a cataloguer thinks about a collection — with consistent identifiers, parallel structure, and a reader who might arrive from any angle.

the seven-p tagline isn't decoration. it's a schema. paper is the physical substrate. pocket is portability. product is the artifact. process is the method. platform is the substrate. pipeline is the throughput. prospects are what the archive exists to protect — the people who haven't shown up yet.

each QR code in the footer is a live node. not a screenshot of past work. not a pdf attached to an email. a working url that you can scan off a printed page and arrive somewhere real. the resume performs its own thesis: that documents should be alive, that links should resolve, that what you made should still be there when someone looks for it.

that's the internet archive's whole religion.

the resume was built inside plan98 — a browser-based OS whose entire architecture is about making things that persist. WAS (Web Address Space) signs files with an Ed25519 key. elves lazy-load only what's needed. the filesystem is addressable. the blog is a first-class citizen. nothing gets thrown away because the design assumes the reader is coming from the future.

the internet archive crawls the web looking for things worth keeping. the resume is already structured like something worth keeping — consistent color keys, live endpoints, a tagline that names the concepts clearly enough to survive outside of context.

the clown on 3-foot stilts walks into the archive and hands them a document that is already an archive of itself. the stilts make it easy to see from across the room.
